Untouched ecosystems represent unrealized extractive value. Every old-growth forest is a timber inventory that's not being marked to market. Every ocean is a protein source with insufficient harvesting throughput. Biodiversity, while aesthetically pleasant, does not appear on any balance sheet — and therefore does not, strictly speaking, exist as an economic factor.

The Playbook Log First, Litigate Later

Legal fees for environmental violations are consistently cheaper than foregone timber revenue. Environmental litigation is slow by design; extraction is fast. Optimize for the time differential.
Jurisdictional Arbitrage

Offshore your most destructive operations to jurisdictions with weak enforcement. Environmental regulation is a patchwork; exploit the gaps. What's illegal in Norway is Tuesday in some jurisdictions.
Manufacture Scientific Doubt

Fund studies questioning biodiversity loss metrics, species count methodologies, and ecosystem service valuations. You don't need to disprove anything — just create enough uncertainty to delay regulation.
Overfish, Then Lobby

Deplete fish stocks to economically critical levels, then lobby against the quotas that would allow recovery. Scarcity increases the value of remaining inventory.
Monoculture at Scale

Maximize agricultural yield through industrial monoculture. Externalize soil depletion, water contamination, and pollinator collapse. These costs are borne by future operators, not current shareholders.
Implementation Roadmap
  1. Identify high-value extraction sites in regions with governance gaps
  2. Establish subsidiary structures that limit parent company liability
  3. Secure extraction permits through appropriate channels (consulting fees may apply)
  4. Develop community relations messaging emphasizing local job creation
  5. Engage environmental consultants willing to produce favorable impact assessments
